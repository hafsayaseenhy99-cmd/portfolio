Section 01 · Initiation
Project Context & Charter
Background, objectives, scope, and project authorisation
HAFSA YASEEN · PM PORTFOLIO

The Challenge: Institute of Business Administration (IBA) Karachi launched a fully-funded BSc Mathematics programme with a reserved quota for public school students. Despite the opportunity, IBA was not receiving sufficient applications from girls in government schools — the talent existed, but the pathway was invisible to them.

The Partnership: IBA approached Pak Alliance for Math and Science — an organisation with strong government school networks in Karachi — to identify, prepare, and support eligible girls to apply and sit the entrance examination. IBA funded the entire programme including venue, transport, exam fees, and facilitator costs.

The Social Reality: Target students came from families living below the poverty line — parents worked as domestic labourers and informal daily-wage workers. Many had never considered university for their daughters. The team's work was as much social as academic: visiting families at home, communicating in Sindhi, dismantling fears about cost and safety, and building the belief that a different future was possible.

Section 03 · Community Mobilization
Community Mobilization Strategy
Aspirational outreach, eligibility screening, and family engagement
HAFSA YASEEN · PM PORTFOLIO

The core challenge was social, not academic. Parents of target students — domestic labourers and informal workers below the poverty line — had never imagined university for their daughters. The team's approach combined aspiration-building with practical problem-solving: showing girls a future they hadn't considered possible, then removing every barrier standing between them and it.

Phase 1: School Visits & Aspirational Workshops

Hafsa and her team visited 6 government schools located near IBA (proximity was a key selection criterion to keep transport cost-effective and safe). In each school, they ran 1-hour aspirational workshops with multiple classes of eligible secondary students, covering:

Component 1: The Vision
Aspiration-building
Showed girls photos of IBA campus, student life, and career paths. Presented maths as a route out of poverty. Built a narrative: work hard now, change your family's future forever.
Component 2: The Opportunity
IBA scholarship explained
Fully-funded BSc Mathematics. Zero cost to families. Exam preparation provided. Transport to and from school covered. No financial barrier to participation.
Component 3: Eligibility Assessment
Bilingual worksheet
Students wrote (in English and Urdu) why they wanted to join, what they understood about the workshop, and answered basic maths questions to assess readiness.
Component 4: Selection
25 girls per school
Top 25 selected per school based on worksheet scores, teacher recommendations, and expressed motivation. 6 schools × 25 = 150 girls enrolled. Waitlist maintained for each school.
Phase 2: Parent Engagement Sessions

In the final two weeks of April, Hafsa and Intern 2 (Sindhi-speaking) conducted parent information meetings at each school. Many parents could only communicate in Sindhi — making language access a critical enabler. The team came down to the parents' level, respected their concerns, and addressed them directly and honestly.

Language access
Intern 2 fluent in Sindhi. Ensured parents fully understood the opportunity, commitment required, and safety arrangements. No parent left the meeting confused.
Addressing fear & refusal
~Dozen parents refused at last minute due to caregiver responsibilities and safety concerns. Girls replaced from school waitlists — enrollment target of 150 maintained.
Long-term framing
Positioned university as the single most powerful tool to change the family's economic trajectory. Spoke directly to parents' deepest aspirations for their daughters.
Waitlist management

A waitlist of additional screened candidates was maintained for each of the 6 schools from the start of the project. When parents refused at the last minute, or girls dropped out during the programme, waitlist candidates were contacted and onboarded. This ensured the project maintained sufficient participant numbers to achieve the 80-exam-sitter target throughout delivery.

Cost breakdown, funding sources, transport logistics, and variance tracking

Budget overview

The programme was fully funded by IBA, which covered all direct costs including venue, transport, facilitator fees, exam costs, and food provision for exam day. Pak Alliance contributed staff time (Hafsa and the two interns). The programme coordinator's role included managing all IBA-allocated expenditure, ensuring funds were disbursed correctly, and reporting spend back to IBA throughout the project.

Transport management

Transport was the most operationally complex element of the budget. IBA funded buses to collect girls from all 6 schools and return them after sessions — critical to achieving high attendance from a population where independent travel raised safety concerns. Hafsa liaised daily with the transport vendor, coordinating schedules for Batch 1 (morning) and Batch 2 (afternoon), managing changes, and ensuring the vendor was briefed on the special exam day requirements at least one week in advance.

Final outcomes
The programme achieved every objective set at initiation. 150 girls from 6 Karachi government schools completed 8 weeks of intensive mathematics preparation. 80 girls sat the IBA entrance examination in July 2023 — many of them the first in their families ever to sit a university entrance exam. The programme proved so successful it was run again in 2024. It demonstrated that girls from the most under-resourced communities, when given aspiration, preparation, transport, and consistent support, can reach for the most prestigious universities in Pakistan.

Lessons learned
1
Language access is a non-negotiable in community programmes
Without a Sindhi-speaking team member, a large portion of parents could not have been engaged. Build linguistic access into every community-facing team from the start.
2
Waitlists are a project management tool, not just a courtesy
Maintaining a screened waitlist per school allowed the team to replace last-minute refusals and dropouts without disrupting programme numbers. Over-recruitment was deliberate, not accidental.
3
Contracted team members need formal accountability structures
University facilitators who were otherwise unresponsive became reliable once formal check-ins and clear expectations were established in writing. Informal assumptions do not hold in complex programmes.
4
Operational logistics make or break community programmes
Transport was the single biggest enabler of attendance. When buses ran reliably, girls came. Vendor management, backup planning, and daily coordination were as important as the academic content.
